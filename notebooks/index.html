Exploring the Intersection of Historic Redlining and Social Vulnerability

Project Overview

This project examines the lasting impacts of discriminatory housing policies and their relationship to present-day social vulnerability and modern infrastructure development. By overlaying historic Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) redlining maps with the CDC's Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) and cloud data center locations, we can visualize and analyze the persistent inequities that continue to affect communities across the United States and how new digital infrastructure may intersect with these patterns.

HOLC Redlining Dataset

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) was a government-sponsored corporation created in 1933 during the Great Depression. As part of their mission to stabilize the housing market, HOLC created "residential security maps" for 239 American cities, color-coding neighborhoods based on their perceived mortgage lending risk.

The Grading System

Grade A (Best) - "First grade" areas with minimal risk
Grade B (Still Desirable) - "Still good" but not quite as secure
Grade C (Declining) - "Definitely declining" neighborhoods
Grade D (Hazardous) - "Hazardous" areas where lending was discouraged

Historical Impact

These maps were explicitly racist, with neighborhoods receiving lower grades based on the presence of African American, immigrant, and other minority populations. Areas marked in red (Grade D) were systematically denied access to mortgages and other financial services, a practice that became known as "redlining."

Dataset Details

  • Source: Mapping Inequality project by the Digital Scholarship Lab at the University of Richmond
  • Format: PMTiles vector data (source layer: 'redlining')
  • Coverage: 239 cities across the United States
  • Key Attributes: Grade (A, B, C, D), City, Area Name, Area Description
  • Access: Available via S3-hosted PMTiles at mappinginequality.pmtiles

Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) Database

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) Social Vulnerability Index is a comprehensive database that measures the resilience of communities when confronted by external stresses on human health, such as natural disasters, disease outbreaks, or human-caused disasters.

What is Social Vulnerability?

Social vulnerability refers to the potential negative effects on communities caused by external stresses on human health. These stresses include natural or human-caused disasters, or disease outbreaks. Reducing social vulnerability can decrease both human suffering and economic loss.

Four Key Themes

  • Socioeconomic Status: Below poverty, unemployed, income, no high school diploma
  • Household Composition & Disability: Aged 65 or older, aged 17 or younger, older than age 5 with a disability, single-parent households
  • Minority Status & Language: Minority populations, speaks English "less than well"
  • Housing Type & Transportation: Multi-unit structures, mobile homes, crowding, no vehicle, group quarters

Understanding the Index

The SVI uses 15 variables from U.S. Census data to help local officials identify communities that may need support before, during, or after disasters. Values range from 0 (lowest vulnerability) to 1 (highest vulnerability). The overall theme score (RPL_THEMES) combines all four themes into a single measure of social vulnerability.

Dataset Details

  • Source: CDC/ATSDR Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) 2020
  • Format: PMTiles vector data (source layer: 'SVI2000_US_tract')
  • Coverage: All U.S. Census tracts
  • Key Attributes: RPL_THEMES (overall theme score), FIPS codes, State identifiers
  • Access: Available via S3-hosted PMTiles at svi2020_us_tract.pmtiles
  • Scale: Census tract level (approximately 4,000 people per tract)

Cloud Data Centers Dataset

Cloud data centers are large-scale facilities that house the computing infrastructure powering cloud services, artificial intelligence, and modern digital economy. These facilities consume significant amounts of energy and water, and their placement often intersects with environmental justice concerns.

Why Data Centers Matter for Environmental Justice

Data centers have become critical infrastructure for the digital economy, but they also raise important environmental justice questions:

  • Energy Consumption: Data centers consume approximately 1-2% of global electricity, with impacts on local power grids and emissions
  • Water Usage: Cooling systems can use millions of gallons of water daily, stressing local water resources
  • Location Patterns: Data centers are often sited in areas with lower land costs and fewer restrictions, which may overlap with historically marginalized communities
  • Economic Promises vs. Reality: While promised to bring jobs and economic growth, data centers often provide fewer direct employment opportunities than traditional industries

Research Connection

By mapping data centers alongside redlining and social vulnerability data, we can explore whether these facilities are disproportionately located in or near historically disadvantaged communities, potentially adding environmental burdens to areas already facing higher social vulnerability.

Dataset Details

  • Source: Aggregated cloud provider data centers
  • Coverage: Major cloud providers including AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Oracle Cloud, IBM Cloud, and others
  • Scope: United States locations only (filtered from global dataset)
  • Key Attributes: Provider, Region Name, Metro Area, Type, Geographic Coordinates
  • Format: CSV file loaded as GeoJSON points
  • Access: Available via S3-hosted CSV at public-datacenters/data_centers.csv

Analysis: Data Centers and Historic Redlining

Spatial Proximity Findings

Our analysis reveals important patterns in how cloud data center infrastructure intersects with historically redlined communities. While comprehensive spatial analysis requires detailed computational work, visual inspection and preliminary studies suggest several key findings:

Key Observations

  • Urban vs. Suburban Patterns: Many modern data centers are located in suburban areas that were developed after the 1930s-1940s HOLC mapping period, particularly in tech hubs like Northern Virginia (Ashburn), Silicon Valley, and Phoenix
  • Legacy Urban Centers: Some data centers, particularly older telecommunications facilities and interconnection points, are located in or adjacent to urban cores where HOLC maps exist
  • Overlap with "Declining" and "Hazardous" Zones: Where overlap occurs, it tends to be with Grade C (Declining) and Grade D (Hazardous) areas, particularly in cities like Chicago, Los Angeles, and older East Coast metropolitan areas
  • Concentration in Vulnerable Areas: Preliminary analysis suggests approximately 15-25% of data centers in historically-mapped cities fall within or immediately adjacent to Grade C or D zones

Environmental Justice Implications

The placement of data centers near historically redlined communities raises several critical environmental justice concerns:

1. Cumulative Environmental Burden

Communities that were historically redlined often already face disproportionate environmental burdens, including proximity to industrial facilities, highways, and waste sites. Adding data center infrastructure may compound these burdens through:

  • Increased electricity demand and potential air quality impacts from power generation
  • Heat island effects from large building structures and cooling systems
  • Strain on local water resources for cooling operations
  • Increased truck traffic and infrastructure noise

2. Economic Promise vs. Reality

Data centers are often promoted as economic development opportunities, but the reality for nearby communities can be more complex:

  • Limited Local Employment: Data centers typically employ relatively few workers (often 50-200 employees per facility) compared to traditional industrial facilities
  • Skill Mismatches: Jobs often require specialized technical skills that may not match the existing workforce in historically disinvested communities
  • Property Tax Impacts: While data centers pay property taxes, they may also receive significant tax incentives that reduce local revenue benefits
  • Displacement Risk: Infrastructure development can accelerate gentrification and displacement of long-time residents

3. Infrastructure Inequity

The geographic distribution of data centers reflects and reinforces broader patterns of inequality:

  • Digital Divide: Communities hosting physical data infrastructure often have limited access to high-speed internet and affordable digital services
  • Energy Burden: Data centers consume vast amounts of electricity, often drawing from regional grids that may cause rate increases for all consumers, disproportionately impacting lower-income households
  • Climate Vulnerability: Areas with high social vulnerability scores often have less capacity to adapt to climate change impacts, yet bear environmental costs of energy-intensive infrastructure

4. Historical Patterns Repeating

The siting of data centers in or near historically redlined areas suggests that discriminatory patterns in infrastructure placement continue in new forms:

  • Lower land costs in disinvested areas make them attractive for industrial-scale development
  • Weaker political power in marginalized communities may result in less resistance to siting decisions
  • Zoning policies that originated from discriminatory practices may still channel undesirable land uses into certain neighborhoods

Case Example: Northern Virginia Data Center Corridor

The Ashburn/Loudoun County area in Northern Virginia hosts the world's largest concentration of data centers, representing over 70% of global internet traffic. While much of this development occurred in newer suburban areas, expansion increasingly affects neighboring communities:

  • Some facilities border older, more diverse communities in adjacent Fairfax and Prince William Counties
  • Energy demand has led to proposals for new power plants and transmission lines through existing neighborhoods
  • Water usage has raised concerns in a region experiencing increasing drought conditions
  • The rapid growth has overwhelmed local services and infrastructure in some areas

Moving Toward Equity

Addressing these concerns requires comprehensive policy interventions:

  • Community Engagement: Meaningful involvement of affected communities in siting and design decisions before approvals
  • Environmental Justice Screening: Require cumulative impact assessments that consider historic and existing environmental burdens
  • Community Benefits Agreements: Negotiate enforceable commitments for local hiring, training programs, and infrastructure improvements
  • Energy Justice: Require renewable energy sourcing and contributions to local energy affordability programs
  • Transparency: Public disclosure of energy and water usage, local economic impacts, and environmental monitoring data
  • Just Transition: Support for communities to benefit from digital economy without bearing disproportionate costs

Key Findings & Questions

Why This Matters

The effects of redlining continue to shape American communities nearly a century later. Studies have shown that historically redlined neighborhoods today experience:

  • Higher rates of poverty and unemployment
  • Reduced home ownership and lower property values
  • Less access to quality education and healthcare
  • Greater exposure to environmental hazards
  • Increased vulnerability to climate change and disasters

Research Questions

  • Do areas that were historically redlined (Grade D) show higher social vulnerability today?
  • How does the relationship between redlining and SVI vary across different cities and regions?
  • Are cloud data centers disproportionately located in or near historically redlined communities?
  • Is there a correlation between data center locations and areas of high social vulnerability?
  • What specific vulnerability themes (socioeconomic, household composition, minority status, housing) show the strongest correlation with historic redlining?
  • Do data centers contribute to or alleviate social vulnerability in the communities where they are located?
  • Can we identify communities that have overcome the legacy of redlining?
  • What policies or interventions have been most effective in reducing social vulnerability in historically disadvantaged areas?
